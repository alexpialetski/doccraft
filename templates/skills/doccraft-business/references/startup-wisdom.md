# Startup Wisdom

Distilled rules of thumb for building, launching, and growing startups, compiled from founder long-form writing.

## Idea Selection

- Work only on problems you understand well enough to judge quality; start in a domain you already use daily.
- Define the domains you will work in and won't; your personal concept is your filter against distraction.
- Reject ideas where you are the only one who thinks the problem exists; no competitors means no market.
- Reject ideas that start from technology capability rather than human need.
- Build on a rising market; a mediocre product on a growing trend beats a great product in a declining one.
- Target top-one or top-two priority problems for your customer, not nice-to-haves.
- Clone a working product with a meaningful tweak — price model, audience, or one key feature — rather than inventing from scratch.
- Choose an idea customers explain in their own words, not one you must educate them to want.
- Filter ideas by existing search demand, existing payment behavior, buildability in weeks, and beatable competition.
- Spend 10–20 hours on market size, competitor, and search-traffic research before committing to an idea.
- Use Google Trends to identify growing problem categories before choosing a niche.
- Confirm that scalable traffic exists before building; no marketer can sell what cold traffic won't buy.
- Enter markets with high daily transaction volume; rare-purchase niches make testing and iteration impossibly slow.
- Treat idea search as a pipeline: funnel dozens of markets down to one testable idea; to find one good idea, screen sixteen candidates.
- Enter competitive markets confidently; each new entrant expands total demand and validates that buyers exist.

## Validation

- Before building, define the customer avatar, their current workaround, and what they already pay for it.
- Validate demand on existing marketplaces or classifieds before writing a line of code; live listings prove people pay.
- Collect payment manually before writing any code; prove demand with a service, then productize.
- Never validate with friends or family; only purchase decisions from paying strangers in your target market are real signal.
- Talk to 20+ potential customers before writing code; confirm the problem is real and acute.
- Launch a landing page and collect payment before building; a sale on an incomplete product confirms the idea.
- A launch counts only when you have a reproducible funnel — targeted impressions → leads → paying customers — at statistically valid volume.
- Judge a project only after driving genuinely targeted traffic; early drop-off and untargeted traffic prove nothing about your product.
- Never conclude from a single ad test; marketing carries 20–50% noise — run multiple attempts before drawing conclusions.
- Draw funnel insights only from statistically meaningful data volume; never make decisions on ten data points.
- Prove one paying customer before thinking about scale.
- Don't trust your gut about market demand; downloads and revenue are the only valid signal.

## Product

- Start with the simplest delivery format; run manually before automating; launch one feature that solves one problem.
- Build only products people need daily or hourly; low-frequency use cases are nearly impossible to monetize.
- Design to deliver maximum result with minimum customer effort; sell the painkiller, not the vitamin.
- Build tools that grow your customer's revenue, not efficiency; revenue tools get budget, efficiency tools get cut.
- Sell the delivered outcome, not the tool; package every product as a solution to a specific problem, not a feature list.
- Ship embarrassingly early; if you are not ashamed of v1, you launched too late.
- Show the paywall after the first result, not before; users who have seen value convert, those who haven't won't.
- Simplify onboarding aggressively: make it mandatory, linear, and end with the exact outcome promised in the ad.
- Remove every registration friction; 25% of paid traffic drops at signup — no captchas, SMS confirmation, or walls of text.
- Focus on retention and recurring payment; target 30-day retention above 20% and monthly subscription renewal above 90%.
- Pivot the solution, not the problem; iterate on the same idea for at least six months before switching direction.
- Build products you use yourself; personal-itch products have better quality and staying power.
- Embed your product link in every output it produces; make the product itself a distribution channel.
- Use the tech stack you already know; spend the time saved on marketing, not learning new frameworks.
- Avoid a full backend until you have paying users; cobble together managed services first and refactor only after the market has spoken.

## Go-to-Market

- Define your offer — the concrete benefit the buyer receives — before spending on any traffic.
- Write landing page headlines as plain descriptions of what you sell; describe customer pain in their own words; use one specific action CTA.
- Selling is non-negotiable at every stage; sell by dramatizing the problem and consequences, not by describing features.
- Spend 80% of time on distribution and promotion, 20% on building; traffic is the business, not the product.
- Build a search-intent funnel first, then social media ads, then organic channels; build the sales funnel before scaling the product.
- Make ads deliberately rough and authentic; ugly creative breaks banner blindness and converts better than polished ads.
- Validate at a small daily budget before scaling; judge paid traffic only after a full sales cycle has closed, not by intermediate metrics.
- Onboard early users manually: contact each signup, fix bugs in real time, gather feedback, and repeat until you have 10 paying customers.
- Launch on distribution channels competitors already use; secure your distribution channel before building the product.
- Build an audience around the problem before launching the product; a warm list converts far better than cold traffic.
- Add referral incentives into the product from day one to turn satisfied users into a zero-cost acquisition channel.
- Collect five-star reviews systematically and continuously; they drive steady free inbound traffic for years.
- Launch in geographies where ad auctions are cheap; compete where CAC is low.
- Publish original research and free tools to earn press links; one backlink from an authoritative source outweighs thousands from unknown sites.
- In B2B, solve your customer's revenue-generation problem first; focus on sales or marketing problems where small businesses feel the sharpest pain.

## Unit Economics

- Focus on net profit from day one; audience without margin is not a business.
- Set full prices from day one; underpricing is a structural flaw no marketing can fix later.
- Price for perceived value, not cost of delivery; price high enough that traffic pays for itself.
- Aim for LTV above $100; low-LTV products rarely cover paid traffic costs.
- Target 4× return on ad spend per quarter; if marketing doesn't clear that bar, fix the product, targeting, or allocation.
- Reinvest 25–30% of revenue into sales and marketing; never spend more on a project than it has earned.
- Push an annual subscription immediately after trial; add upsells from day one to shorten break-even.
- Identify who actually pays, not who uses the product; engagement without payment is a vanity metric.
- Accept 3% funnel conversion as normal; raise price or scale volume instead of chasing higher rates.
- Build for profitability from day one so revenue funds growth and you avoid repeated dilutive fundraising.
- Scale only what has already been confirmed to work financially; validate unit economics before automating or raising.
- In API-reseller products, expect 30–50% of revenue consumed by upstream costs; price accordingly from the start.

## Fundraising

- Raise outside funding only after reaching a repeatable revenue base; pre-traction investment hands away equity for marginal help.
- Avoid venture funding if your goal is personal profit; VC math optimizes for unicorn outcomes, not lifestyle businesses.
- Don't raise seed funding just to launch; validate with cheap tools first, raise only after you have paying customers and a working funnel.
- Accelerators cost 7–15% equity for three months of meetings; spend those months getting real sales instead.
- Build your own revenue-generating product so a layoff can't kill your income.

## Team

- Pair one person who sells with one person who builds; clear ownership and fast communication beat every other early-stage structure.
- Use solo founding only to test a hypothesis; find a co-founder before you must run marketing, sales, and product simultaneously.
- A co-founder matters more than a great idea; they keep you from quitting before you finish.
- Only take product and business advice from people who have personally risked money launching products.
- Sell through the person who already sells to your target user; partner with coaches and experts who have warm audiences.

## Founder Mindset

- Start your side project now; obligations only multiply with age, making a future start harder.
- Dedicate at least two focused hours daily; consistent hours compound faster than sporadic bursts.
- Keep your day job while building; financial pressure slows iteration and time constraints force faster decisions.
- An idea is worthless without execution; protect your energy, not your concept.
- Persist through the first sales drought with iteration, not abandonment; nearly every eventual success required multiple pivots.
- Analyze every failed attempt on paper before starting the next; repeating launches without reflection scales your mistakes.
- Expect three to twelve months before a meaningful revenue system; plan for one to two years to reach micro-business scale.
- Ship more projects; volume of attempts converts to quality outcomes faster than waiting for the perfect idea.
- Choose a project you genuinely care about; enjoyment is what sustains daily work when results are slow.
- Complete every step fully before moving to the next; partial execution is the primary reason projects fail.
- Focus on one bottleneck at a time — idea, traffic, product, or customers — never all at once simultaneously.
- The risk of inaction now exceeds the risk of launching; a failed attempt costs a weekend and minimal budget.
- AI multiplies existing skills; invest in genuine depth before technical skills are commoditized.
- Learn marketing and distribution now; distribution judgment outlasts any technical advantage.
- In every market downturn, ad costs drop and weak competitors exit; treat crises as opportunities, not threats.
